# AAM — American Alliance of Museums

_Reference Note for MCOS — 2026-06-21_

---

## What Is AAM?

The [American Alliance of Museums](https://www.aam-us.org/) (AAM) is the primary professional association for museums in the United States. Founded in 1906 (as the American Association of Museums; renamed in 2012), it represents the full spectrum of US museums — art, history, science, children's, natural history, zoos, aquariums, botanical gardens, and more — across all sizes and budget levels. AAM advocates for the museum field at the federal level, publishes professional standards and ethics guidance, and administers the field's primary accreditation program.

---

## The Continuum of Excellence

AAM describes its professional development and recognition programs as a **Continuum of Excellence** — a tiered set of programs that any museum, regardless of size or budget, can participate in:

| Program | What It Is |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Pledge of Excellence** | Entry-level commitment to AAM's Core Standards; a public declaration of intent |
| **Museum Assessment Program (MAP)** | Peer-reviewed self-assessment across four assessment types: Organizational, Collections Stewardship, Community Engagement, and Board Leadership |
| **Collections Assessment for Preservation (CAP)** | Collections care and conservation assessment; administered by the Foundation for Advancement in Conservation (FAIC) with AAM support |
| **Standards for Excellence for History Organizations (STEPs)** | Standards specifically for historical societies and history museums |
| **Core Documents Verification** | Formal verification that a museum has the key governance documents AAM requires |
| **Accreditation** | Full peer-review accreditation — the top tier of the Continuum |

---

## AAM Accreditation

The AAM Accreditation Program has been the field's mark of museum excellence since **1971** and is widely described as the gold standard for US museums. The process combines self-study and peer review and takes 8–16 months to complete, with reaccreditation required every ten years.

Of an estimated **33,000 museums** in the United States, approximately **1,125 are currently accredited** — roughly 3%. Accreditation signals to funders, donors, government agencies, and the public that a museum meets rigorous professional standards and is an appropriate steward of the collections held in public trust.

## Core Standards for Museums

AAM's **Core Standards** are the field-wide baseline — broad, outcome-oriented statements that all museums, accredited or not, are expected to meet. They are organized into seven areas:

| Area | What It Covers |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Public Trust & Accountability** | Operates for public benefit; transparent governance; ethical practices |
| **Mission & Planning** | Clear mission statement; strategic planning tied to mission |
| **Leadership & Organizational Structure** | Effective governing authority; qualified professional leadership |
| **Collections Stewardship** | Legal, ethical, and effective management, documentation, care, and use of collections |
| **Education & Interpretation** | Fulfills educational mission; provides meaningful public experiences |
| **Financial Stability** | Sound financial management; revenue diversity; long-term planning |
| **Facilities & Risk Management** | Safe, accessible facilities; emergency planning; risk management |

## Collections Stewardship Standards

The Collections Stewardship area of the Core Standards is the most directly relevant to MCOS. AAM defines stewardship as *the careful, sound, and responsible management of that which is entrusted to a museum's care*. The stewardship standards require that museums:

- Own, exhibit, or use collections appropriate to their mission
- Legally, ethically, and effectively **manage, document, care for, and use** collections
- Conduct collections-related research according to appropriate scholarly standards
- Strategically plan for collection use and development
- Provide **public access** to collections while ensuring their preservation
- Maintain appropriate care policies for objects on exhibition, in storage, on loan, and in transit

The *National Standards and Best Practices for U.S. Museums* (AAM, 2008; Elizabeth E. Merritt, ed.) spells out the field expectation at the object-record level: every object should have a **unique accession number, title, provenance, rights statement, and condition record**. These five fields are the minimum documentation baseline referenced in MCOS's standards alignment.

## AAM Code of Ethics for Museums

The [AAM Code of Ethics](https://www.aam-us.org/programs/ethics-standards-and-professional-practices/code-of-ethics-for-museums/) addresses the ethical obligations of museums as public trusts. Key provisions relevant to collections management:

- **Public trust** — Collections are held in trust for the public benefit, not for the institution's or any individual's private gain
- **Collections acquisition** — Museums must be able to demonstrate clear legal title to acquired objects; ethical acquisition avoids items obtained in violation of the 1970 UNESCO Convention on cultural property
- **Deaccessioning** — Proceeds from the sale of deaccessioned objects must be used only for collections acquisition or direct care of the collection
- **Preservation** — Museums have an obligation to preserve and protect the objects in their care
- **Access** — Museums must provide public access to their collections to the greatest extent possible consistent with preservation

## AAM and Core Documents

AAM identifies a set of **core governance documents** that every museum should maintain. These are required for accreditation and strongly recommended for all museums:

| Document | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- |
| Mission statement | Defines the museum's public purpose |
| Institutional code of ethics | Governs staff and board conduct |
| Strategic or long-range plan | Guides institutional direction |
| Collections management policy | Governs acquisition, care, access, deaccessioning, and loans |
| Disaster preparedness / emergency plan | Protects collections and staff in emergencies |

The **Collections Management Policy** is the document most relevant to MCOS: it defines the institution's rules for accession numbers, provenance documentation, condition recording, rights statements, loan procedures, and deaccessioning — the same fields and workflows MCOS implements.

## AAM and MCOS

MCOS aligns with AAM's Core Standards at the schema and workflow level. The MCOS object record is designed so that a museum using it can demonstrate AAM-standard collections documentation:

| AAM Requirement | MCOS Implementation |
| :--- | :--- |
| Unique accession number | Required field on every object record; enforced at accession |
| Title | Required field |
| Provenance | Dedicated provenance field on every object record |
| Rights statement | Required field; public IA publishing is blocked without a declared rights statement |
| Condition record | Condition grade, condition notes, and condition assessment date on every object record |
| Public access | Internet Archive publishing built in from Phase 1 |
| Audit trail | Complete change history with user attribution and before/after field values on every record |
| Loans in / Loans out | Full loan agreement module with condition-out/condition-in recording (Phase 2) |

MCOS does not claim AAM accreditation compliance — accreditation evaluates the whole institution, not just its software. However, a museum using MCOS can demonstrate that its collections documentation meets the minimum field requirements that AAM accreditation reviewers look for.
